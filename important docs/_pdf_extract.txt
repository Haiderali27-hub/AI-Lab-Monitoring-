Project Description
The AI-Driven Lab Exam Monitoring and Management System is a secure exam-
ination platform designed for university computer laboratories. The system provides
controlled exam execution, real-time monitoring, AI-assisted evaluation, and plagiarism
detection within a unified framework.
It enforces authorized device access, restricts unauthorized applications through a
lockdown mechanism, collects monitoring evidence during exams, and automates the
grading for MCQ, coding, and theory-based assessments.
The proposed solution aims to improve fairness, efficiency, and academic integrity in
laboratory-based examinations.
1 Introduction
With the increasing digitalization of higher education, universities are conducting a
growing number of examinations in computer laboratories. These exams often involve
programming, database management, and other technical tasks that require controlled
system access and strict supervision.
Despite being conducted in a controlled environment, lab-based examinations face sev-
eral challenges. Students may attempt to access unauthorized applications, switch tasks,
use external storage devices, or collaborate unfairly. Manual invigilation alone is often
insufficient to monitor all students effectively, especially in large classes. Additionally,
grading technical exams manually is time-consuming and may lead to inconsistencies.
Detecting plagiarism within the same class is also difficult without automated analysis
tools.
These limitations highlight the need for a secure, intelligent, and integrated system
tailored specifically for laboratory examination environments.
This proposal presents the design and development of an AI-Driven Lab Exam Mon-
itoring and Management System that aims to enhance examination security, automate
evaluation, and promote academic integrity in university lab settings.
2 Application/Literature Review
Several examination and proctoring systems have been developed to manage digital
exams and reduce cheating. However, most existing solutions focus either on online quiz
delivery, browser-level lockdown, or remote proctoring. They typically do not provide a
unified, lab-focused system that combines operating system restrictions, evidence collec-
tion, AI-assisted evaluation, and integrated reporting.
1
Moodle Quiz [1]is a widely used Learning Management System (LMS) feature that
supports online quizzes and assessments. It provides tools for quiz scheduling, question
banks, grading for objective questions, and reports.
Features:
•Quiz creation and scheduling
•Question bank management
•Automatic grading for MCQs
•Time limits and attempt control
•Basic reports and statistics
Limitations:
•No operating-system level lockdown for lab machines
•Limited real-time monitoring during the exam
•Does not natively restrict desktop applications (e.g., compiler-only access)
•Limited support for automated evaluation of coding problems
Safe Exam Browser (SEB) [2]is a secure browser environment used for e-assessments.
It turns a computer into a restricted workstation by controlling access to system functions,
websites, and applications during an exam.
Features:
•Browser-based lockdown
•Restricts access to websites and system functions
•Can restrict applications based on configuration
•Used with LMS systems such as Moodle
Limitations:
•Monitoring evidence (screenshots/webcam) is not a complete built-in solution
•No AI-assisted evaluation for coding/theory exams
•No integrated plagiarism detection and reporting
Respondus LockDown Browser [3]is another exam security tool that locks down
the testing environment within an LMS. When enabled, students are prevented from
printing, copying, accessing other applications, or navigating away until the exam is
submitted.
Features:
2
•Locks down the exam environment within supported LMS platforms
•Prevents switching to other applications during the exam
•Restricts printing, copying, and other actions
•Optional webcam-based monitoring through companion tools
Limitations:
•Primarily designed for browser-based exams, not full lab workflows
•Limited integration of AI grading for coding/theory exams
•Plagiarism reporting is not the core focus and may require external tools
ProctorU [4]is an online proctoring service designed mainly for remote examina-
tions. It supports identity verification, secure browser usage, and live/recorded monitor-
ing to reduce cheating.
Features:
•Live and/or recorded remote proctoring
•Identity verification and session monitoring
•Incident reporting and exam-day guidance
Limitations:
•Focused on remote exams rather than controlled lab environments
•Cost and privacy concerns for some institutions
•Limited operating-system level control in a lab-specific setup
Based on the reviewed systems, there is a gap for a laboratory-focused solution that
combines strict lockdown control, continuous evidence collection, AI-assisted evaluation,
and integrated plagiarism detection within a single platform.
3 Problem Statement
Universities increasingly conduct technical examinations in computer laboratories for
programming, database, and software-related courses [9]. Although these exams are held
in controlled environments, maintaining strict academic integrity remains a significant
challenge.
During lab-based examinations, students may attempt to switch applications, access
unauthorized software, use external storage devices, or collaborate with peers [8]. Manual
invigilation alone may not effectively detect subtle violations, especially in large classes

the risk of unfair practices and reduces trust in the examination process.
Furthermore, grading coding and theory-based exams manually is time-consuming
and prone to inconsistencies. In large classes, evaluating each submission individually
requires significant effort from instructors. Additionally, detecting plagiarism among
students within the same class is difficult without automated similarity analysis tools.
Automated plagiarism detection systems such as MOSS [5] have been widely adopted in

studies of source-code plagiarism detection engines [6, 7] highlight the importance of

Existing solutions such as LMS-based quiz systems, browser-level lockdown tools,
or remote proctoring services address only partial aspects of the problem. They often
lack full operating system-level control in laboratory environments, integrated AI-assisted
evaluation for coding and theory exams, and comprehensive reporting with audit trails.
The unmet need is therefore a unified, laboratory-focused examination management
system that enforces strict system control, provides real-time monitoring, automates eval-
uation, and detects plagiarism within a single integrated platform.
The primary stakeholders for such a system include universities, examination de-
partments, faculty members, and academic institutions conducting technical lab-based
assessments. As higher education institutions continue to digitize examination processes,
the demand for secure and intelligent lab examination systems is expected to grow sig-
nificantly.

4 Project Aim and Objectives
Project Aim:
The primary aim of this project is to design and implement a secure, intelligent, and
integrated laboratory examination management system that ensures controlled exam ex-
ecution, real-time monitoring, automated evaluation, and academic integrity enforcement
within university computer labs.
Value Proposition:
Unlike existing solutions that focus either on online quiz management, browser-level
restriction, or remote proctoring, the proposed system provides a unified laboratory-
focused architecture. It combines operating system-level lockdown, continuous moni-
toring, AI-assisted grading, and intra-class plagiarism detection into a single integrated
platform specifically designed for controlled lab environments.

restrictions.

•Develop AI-assisted evaluation for MCQ, coding, and theory-based assessments.
•Implement intra-class plagiarism detection using similarity analysis techniques.
•Provide a teacher dashboard for live proctoring and administrative control.
•Generate structured reports, audit logs, and analytics for transparency and review.
•Ensure system scalability, reliability, and security for multiple lab environments.
Final Project Output:
The final deliverable of this project will include:
•A desktop-based student examination application (Windows-based lab environ-

•A web-based teacher and administrator control panel.

6 Project Development Methodology
The proposed system will be developed using an iterative and modular development
approach. The project will be divided into structured phases including analysis, design,
implementation, testing, and integration. Each core module will be developed and tested
independently before system-level integration.
Development Approach
An iterative development model will be adopted to ensure continuous improvement
and early detection of issues. Each module will be implemented as a separate component
and validated before integration with other modules. This approach ensures flexibility,
better debugging, and progressive system refinement.

7 Tools and Technologies
The implementation of the AI-Driven Lab Exam Monitoring and Management System
will involve a combination of desktop application development, web technologies, backend
services, and AI-based processing tools.
1. Student Examination Application:The student-side examination client will
be developed using C# with .NET (WPF or WinForms). This platform is chosen because
it provides strong integration with Windows-based laboratory systems and allows operat-
ing system-level control required for implementing lockdown mechanisms and application
restrictions.
2. Backend API Server:ASP.NET Core will be used to develop a RESTful API
for secure communication between the student application, teacher panel, and database
server. It provides scalability, performance, and built-in security features such as authen-
tication and role-based access control.
3. Teacher and Admin Web Panel:The web-based dashboard will be developed
using ASP.NET Core with Razor Pages or a modern frontend framework (such as React,
if required). This interface will allow teachers to create exams, monitor students, and
generate reports.
4. Database Management System:SQL Server or PostgreSQL will be used as the
relational database system to store user data, exam configurations, monitoring records,
submissions, and logs. A relational database is suitable due to the structured nature of
examination data.
5. AI and Similarity Analysis Module:Python may be integrated as a microser-
vice for implementing similarity analysis and automated grading algorithms. Modern
AI-based evaluation approaches such as Deep Knowledge Tracing [10] demonstrate how
machine learning can model student performance patterns. Similarly, clustering-based
grading techniques [11] show that automated short-answer and structured-response grad-
ing can reduce instructor workload while maintaining consistency.
10
6. Communication and Security:Secure HTTP (HTTPS) protocols will be used
for data transmission between clients and servers. Authentication tokens and encrypted
session handling will ensure secure access control.
7. Development and Version Control Tools:Visual Studio will be used as the
primary development environment. Git will be used for version control and collaborative
development among team members.
This combination of technologies ensures secure system-level control, scalable backend
processing, structured data management, and reliable deployment within a university-
controlled laboratory environment.

Project Overview
The proposed project, AI-Driven Lab Exam Monitoring and Management System, ad-
dresses the need for a controlled and well-managed environment for practical laboratory
examinations conducted in universities and other academic institutions. In courses such
as programming, databases, software engineering, networking, and operating systems,
students are assessed on institutional machines where academic integrity depends on
secure access, proper scheduling, controlled workstation usage, continuous monitoring,
organized submission handling, and dependable reporting. Existing tools usually address
only one part of this process, such as browser lockdown, online quiz delivery, plagiarism
detection, or grading support, but they do not provide one integrated workflow specifically
designed for in-person lab examinations.
The scope of the project is to develop a unified academic platform that supports exam
scheduling, user and role management, lab allocation, student authentication, face recog-
nition support, AI-assisted monitoring, evidence logging, submission collection, result
management, and report generation. The system is intended to reduce manual invigila-
tion burden, improve institutional visibility during live exams, and maintain a stronger
evidence trail for post-exam review. By combining these functions in one platform, the
project aims to improve fairness, consistency, and accountability in practical assessments.
The development approach of the project is iterative and modular because the solu-
tion contains administrator, teacher, and student workflows along with backend services,
database support, monitoring functions, and reporting modules. The expected outcome
is a practical prototype that can help institutions manage the complete lifecycle of a lab
exam more effectively, from pre-exam planning to post-exam analysis, while also serving
as a foundation for future academic process automation.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The growing dependence of higher education on digital infrastructure has transformed
not only classroom delivery but also the way assessment is planned, conducted, and
evaluated. In computer science and software engineering programs, practical laboratory
examinations occupy a central place because they test problem solving, coding abil-
ity, system usage skills, and real-time decision-making in controlled academic settings.
However, many institutions still conduct such examinations through fragmented work-
flows in which exam scheduling, laboratory readiness, invigilation, student attendance,
suspicious-activity monitoring, submission collection, and post-exam reporting are han-
dled using separate manual or semi-digital methods. This fragmentation reduces opera-
tional clarity and creates avoidable pressure on instructors and invigilators during already
time-sensitive exam sessions. For this reason, the proposed AI-Driven Lab Exam Moni-
toring and Management System has been conceived as an integrated academic platform
that improves visibility, control, traceability, and institutional reliability throughout the
full life cycle of a laboratory examination.
The idea of combining examination management with monitoring support emerges
from a practical institutional need. In many university labs, supervisors are required to
observe multiple students simultaneously, enforce timing rules, answer operational issues,
verify candidate identity, track attendance, and later justify the validity of the conducted
exam. When these responsibilities are distributed across paper lists, messaging groups,
manual observation, local file collection, and after-the-fact reporting, the academic pro-
cess becomes difficult to standardize. Errors are more likely to occur, evidence is harder
to retain, and the fairness of the assessment may become difficult to demonstrate. A
unified digital system can therefore provide an important institutional benefit by con-
necting planning, monitoring, secure execution, submission handling, and reporting into
one auditable workflow.
1.1 Product Scope
The scope of the proposed AI-Driven Lab Exam Monitoring and Management Sys-
tem is limited to the planning, controlled execution, monitoring, submission handling,
and reporting of practical laboratory examinations in an academic environment. The
proposed system covers twelve project modules: user authentication, role management,
exam scheduling, question and instruction management, laboratory allocation, worksta-
tion control, student attendance, face recognition, AI-based monitoring, incident logging,
result management, and reporting. Within this scope, the product delivers five core fea-
ture groups: secure role-based access for administrators, teachers, and students; scheduled
exam creation with lab and machine mapping; monitored exam execution with face ver-
ification and AI-supported alerts; organized submission, result, and evidence handling;
and administrative reporting for review, audit, and decision support.

1.1.2 Future System Usage Analysis
The future system is expected to be used as an operational platform across the com-
plete life cycle of practical laboratory examinations. Before the exam, administrators
and teachers will use it to configure user roles, define examination sessions, assign labora-
tories, map students to approved machines, publish instructions, and confirm readiness.
During the exam, students will use it to access the scheduled assessment under con-
trolled conditions, while teachers and invigilators will rely on the monitoring dashboard
to observe status changes, review alerts, and intervene when necessary. After the exam,

teachers and administrators will use it to review submissions, examine incidents, verify
attendance, manage results, and generate structured reports.
In a practical institutional deployment, the system will be especially valuable in
courses involving programming, software construction, data structures, operating sys-
tems, database laboratories, networking exercises, and related disciplines where each
student works at a computer and produces digital outputs. It can help standardize op-
erational conduct across sections and semesters and reduce dependence on improvised
processes.
Another important future-use dimension concerns accountability and evidence. The
proposed system will support a disciplined evidence trail through structured logs and
linked session records, strengthening institutional memory and supporting academic dis-
putes, result review, and quality evaluation.
1.2 Objectives
The overall objective of the AI-Driven Lab Exam Monitoring and Management System
is to design and implement an integrated platform that improves the planning, supervi-
sion, control, and post-exam accountability of practical laboratory examinations. This
overall objective is operationalized through a set of specific objectives that guide both
the software solution and the academic direction of the project.
The first objective is to digitize and centralize the administration of practical lab-
oratory examinations so that scheduling, user management, laboratory allocation, and
session configuration can be handled consistently from one platform. The second objec-
tive is to provide invigilators and instructors with a stronger real-time awareness of the
ongoing exam through status monitoring, anomaly alerts, and organized session visibility.
The third objective is to create a dependable submission and evidence-handling workflow
so that academic decisions can later be supported through structured records instead of
improvised documentation.
The fourth objective is to improve fairness and procedural reliability by enforcing
controlled access, time-bound execution, and consistent handling of student activity in
the lab environment. The fifth objective is to reduce the burden of manual coordination
placed on instructors and administrators by automating recurring operational tasks and
by presenting consolidated reporting after exam completion. The sixth objective is to
develop the project in a way that is academically meaningful, technically feasible within
FYP constraints, and extensible for future institutional use.
1.3 Problem Statement / Limitations
Practical laboratory examinations are often conducted in environments where the
technical challenge of the assessment is matched by an equally serious management chal-
lenge. In many institutions, there is no single platform that coordinates scheduling,
invigilation, access control, workstation-level tracking, incident recording, and post-exam
reporting in a unified way. As a result, important parts of the process remain manual,
fragmented, or weakly documented. This makes the examination workflow harder to
supervise and more difficult to justify when concerns arise.
One of the most significant limitations of the current environment is the limited ability
of invigilators to monitor many students simultaneously with confidence. In a crowded
computer lab, human attention alone is often insufficient to maintain real-time awareness
of who has started, who is inactive, who may be experiencing technical issues, and who
may be deviating from expected behavior. Even when invigilators are experienced, the
lack of a consolidated dashboard makes consistent situational awareness difficult.

A second limitation concerns evidence and traceability. In many practical exams,
suspicious activity, submission problems, or timing disputes are difficult to reconstruct
after the session has ended. Without structured logs and organized incident records,
academic decisions may depend on fragmented observations rather than well-preserved
evidence. A third limitation lies in the administrative workload caused by separate tools
and manual coordination. Scheduling, attendance, monitoring, submission collection, and
reporting may each require different artifacts or systems, increasing operational overhead
and the possibility of inconsistency.
The proposed project itself also has development limitations. As an FYP, it will
emphasize a well-structured prototype and academically defensible design rather than
claiming full enterprise-scale deployment across all university departments. Advanced
AI-driven anomaly detection may initially be represented through configurable monitor-
ing logic and alert support rather than through a production-trained predictive model.
Similarly, large-scale live deployment constraints such as network variability, institutional
integration complexity, and long-term maintenance policies may remain outside the im-
mediate prototype boundary. These limitations are acknowledged so that the project
remains realistic while still delivering meaningful academic and operational value.
1.4 Benefits of Proposed System
The proposed system offers a set of integrated capabilities that directly address the
operational difficulties of practical laboratory examinations. Role-based user manage-
ment supports different workflows for administrators, teachers, invigilators, and students,
which helps the institution align permissions with responsibilities and reduce unautho-
rized access to sensitive exam functions.
Exam creation and scheduling allow authorized academic staff to define practical
exams, assign dates and durations, publish instructions, and map sessions to relevant
users and labs. This improves planning consistency and reduces confusion before the
session begins.
Lab and workstation control strengthens the connection between the digital work-
flow and the physical laboratory environment by associating sessions with approved ma-
chines and locations. This is especially important in practical exams, where the technical
workspace matters as much as the question paper itself.
Secure exam execution gives students a controlled and time-bound environment that
is aligned with the scheduled session. This reduces ambiguity in exam start and finish
conditions and supports procedural fairness.
The live monitoring dashboard gives invigilators a centralized view of active students,
session status, timing, and alerts. This directly addresses the supervision challenges that
emerge when many students are simultaneously working in the lab.
Alerting and evidence logging preserve meaningful exam events and suspicious occur-
rences as structured evidence. This improves post-exam review and strengthens account-
ability.
Submission management organizes student work through a controlled workflow that
links each file or output with the correct candidate and session. This reduces the risk of
disorganized file collection and supports cleaner evaluation.
Instructor review and administrative reporting help departments review attendance,
incidents, session histories, and submission records through consolidated reporting fea-
tures. This improves institutional documentation and helps departments learn from each
conducted session.

1.7 Software Process Model
The development of the AI-Driven Lab Exam Monitoring and Management System
follows the Agile process model. Agile is particularly suitable for this project because
the system contains several interdependent modules, requires continuous refinement of
workflows, and must respond to user feedback related to usability, security, and academic
practicality. In projects involving dashboards, access roles, examination rules, event
logging, and post-exam reporting, it is difficult to finalize every detail correctly at the
very beginning. Agile provides a controlled but iterative way to build the system in
increments, validate assumptions early, and refine the product across repeated feedback
cycles.
1.7.1 Process Model Introduction
Agile is an iterative and incremental development model in which software evolves
through repeated short cycles called sprints. Each sprint focuses on a manageable subset
of work and produces a meaningful improvement in the system. Rather than postpon-
ing user feedback until the final stage, Agile encourages continuous interaction between
planning, implementation, review, and refinement. This makes it highly appropriate for
academic systems where stakeholders often understand their needs more clearly after
seeing early prototypes and working workflows.
In the context of the proposed system, Agile supports a practical balance between
technical implementation and institutional feedback. An early sprint can validate whether
the role-based dashboard structure is clear, while a later sprint can refine monitoring logic,
face verification flow, or reporting outputs based on observed weaknesses. Such iterative
evolution helps reduce the risk of investing too much effort in features that are technically
complete but operationally unsuitable. It also helps the project remain realistic within
semester constraints while still demonstrating meaningful progress [1] [2].

1.7.2 Justification of Proposing the Process Model
Agile is chosen over a rigid sequential model because the proposed system is not
a single-purpose utility; it is a multi-role academic platform whose usefulness depends
heavily on workflow suitability. A strictly linear process would assume that scheduling
logic, monitoring interface needs, alert behavior, evidence presentation, and reporting
expectations can all be finalized with certainty before implementation begins. In reality,
these design decisions benefit from iterative refinement. A dashboard that appears clear
on paper may prove cluttered in a simulated invigilation scenario. A submission flow
that seems simple in design may become confusing when aligned with real timing rules.
Agile offers a way to respond to such issues before they become deeply embedded in the
system.
Agile is also appropriate because the FYP itself develops under academic review
conditions. Supervisor input, testing observations, and prototype demonstrations all
contribute to the maturity of the final result. Instead of viewing change as a disruption,
Agile treats controlled change as a normal and productive part of development. This
aligns well with a student project in which requirements become sharper through research,
modeling, and demonstration. Furthermore, the modular nature of the proposed system
means that high-priority features such as login, scheduling, monitoring, and evidence
logging can be developed first, while secondary enhancements can be refined later without
destabilizing the project.
1.7.3 Steps of Process Model
The Agile process model for this project is organized into eight sprints. Each sprint
spans four weeks, which is treated as an approximately thirty-day working cycle for plan-
ning, implementation, review, and refinement. The twelve project modules are distributed
across these eight sprints so that each increment produces a meaningful and reviewable
outcome.
Sprint 1 (Weeks 1–4):Requirement Analysis and System Planning. This sprint
establishes the project backlog and defines the architecture baseline. The major modules
covered are requirement analysis and project planning. Activities include stakeholder
discussion, use-case identification, product backlog preparation, initial workflow sketches,
and approval of the project scope.
Sprint 2 (Weeks 5–8):User Authentication and Role Management. This sprint
focuses on secure access to the platform. The modules delivered are user authentication
and role management. Activities include login design, account creation rules, session
handling, access permissions, and dashboard routing for administrators, teachers, and
students.
Sprint 3 (Weeks 9–12):Exam Scheduling and Question Management. This sprint
addresses academic setup before the live exam. The modules delivered are exam schedul-
ing and question or instruction management. Activities include exam creation, date and
duration assignment, course mapping, instruction upload, and controlled publication of
scheduled sessions.
Sprint 4 (Weeks 13–16):Laboratory Allocation and Workstation Control. This
sprint develops the physical-lab administration layer. The modules delivered are labo-
ratory allocation and workstation control. Activities include machine registration, lab
mapping, seat assignment logic, approved-device association, and readiness checks before
exam launch.
Sprint 5 (Weeks 17–20):Student Attendance and Face Recognition. This sprint
strengthens candidate verification. The modules delivered are student attendance and

face recognition. Activities include attendance marking, pre-exam identity confirmation,
candidate image matching flow, and validation of authorized student access to the as-
signed exam session.
Sprint 6 (Weeks 21–24):AI Monitoring and Incident Logging. This sprint covers
active invigilation support during the exam. The modules delivered are AI-based mon-
itoring and incident logging. Activities include live status tracking, suspicious-activity
detection rules, alert creation, evidence capture, timestamped event records, and teacher-

Sprint 7 (Weeks 25–28):Result Management and Reporting. This sprint handles

consolidates all previously completed work into one stable prototype. Activities include
module integration, usability improvement, bug fixing, end-to-end testing, sprint review
closure, documentation completion, and preparation of final academic deliverables for
demonstration and report submission.

SPECIFICATION
The Software Requirements Specification (SRS) defines the expected behaviour, quality
attributes, user constraints, and operational boundaries of the proposed AI-Driven Lab
Exam Monitoring and Management System. In academic software projects, the SRS
performs a critical role because it transforms a general idea into a verifiable and structured
description of what the product must accomplish. For this project, the SRS is especially
important because the system is not limited to a simple examination portal. It must
coordinate scheduling, restricted access, live supervision, evidence recording, controlled
submission, instructor review, and administrative reporting in one coherent environment.
The specification also creates a shared understanding between the student develop-
ment team, academic supervisor, and future stakeholders who may review the project. A
well-written SRS reduces ambiguity by explaining not only which functions should exist,
but also how those functions relate to institutional needs such as fairness, accountability,
transparency, and operational continuity. In the context of lab examinations, unclear
requirements can lead to serious problems. For example, if timing rules are not clearly
defined, students may receive unequal working conditions. If monitoring requirements are
not specified, suspicious incidents may be handled inconsistently. If reporting require-
ments are vague, academic staff may lack the evidence needed to interpret examination
outcomes responsibly.
This chapter therefore explains the scope, audience, functional requirements, non-
functional requirements, requirement elicitation techniques, and project time frame in a
structured manner. The intention is to document the proposed system in sufficient depth
so that later design and implementation decisions remain traceable to academic needs
rather than being driven only by technical convenience. The chapter also supports future
verification because every major feature described here can be translated into design
elements, interface components, or test cases in later phases of the project.

project more complex than a conventional classroom application or static record-keeping
tool.

manner that is structured, transparent, and operationally manageable. Traditional lab-

so that the intended product behaviour is clearer.
2.2.1 User and Access Management Requirements
The proposed system must support structured user administration because the aca-
demic examination process depends on role separation. Administrators manage users
and configuration rules. Teachers create and review exams. Invigilators supervise ongo-
ing sessions. Students participate in the examination and upload their work. If these
responsibilities are not separated properly, the exam environment can become confusing
or open to misuse. Therefore, account creation, role assignment, permission control, and
secure authentication are fundamental requirements rather than optional convenience
features.
CHAPTER 2. SOFTWARE REQUIREMENTS SPECIFICATION17
The user-management component must also preserve operational simplicity. Aca-
demic environments often involve users with different technical skill levels. The system
should therefore make it easy to activate or deactivate accounts, assign users to courses
or exam sessions, and verify access privileges before an exam begins. These functions
reduce the risk of confusion on exam day and strengthen institutional control.
2.2.2 Examination Planning and Scheduling Requirements
A major requirement of the system is the ability to plan practical examinations in an
organized and policy-aligned manner. Authorized users should be able to define exam ti-
tles, descriptions, practical tasks, time limits, start and end windows, permitted locations,
and related instructional details. This planning capability is essential because practical
exams differ widely from one course to another. Some may require coding tasks, others
software tool usage, and others file-based outputs. The system therefore needs scheduling
flexibility without losing procedural discipline.
Scheduling requirements also include the ability to associate an exam with a specific
laboratory, candidate group, or workstation context. In many institutions, different sec-
tions may share labs or conduct sessions at different times. Without structured scheduling
support, such arrangements become difficult to manage fairly. The scheduling functions
must therefore provide enough information for both teachers and invigilators to under-
stand who is appearing, where they are appearing, and under what timing constraints.
2.2.3 Monitoring and Supervision Requirements
Monitoring is one of the defining features of the proposed system. Unlike ordinary
learning platforms that focus mainly on content delivery or grading, this project aims to
strengthen visibility during live practical exams. The system must therefore provide in-
vigilators with a monitoring dashboard that displays active student sessions, timing infor-
mation, progress state, and noteworthy alerts. This dashboard should support situational
awareness rather than forcing invigilators to depend entirely on physical observation.
The monitoring requirement also includes the ability to register suspicious or irregular
events. Depending on the final prototype implementation, such events may include inac-
tivity anomalies, session interruptions, unauthorized state changes, or other configurable
behaviours. The system should not make irreversible judgments automatically, but it
should highlight events that deserve human review. In this way, the monitoring feature
acts as a decision-support mechanism rather than a rigid disciplinary instrument.
2.2.4 Submission and Evidence Requirements
Practical exams often involve source files, outputs, project artifacts, screenshots, or
documents that must be collected in a controlled manner. The system must therefore
provide a clear submission workflow so that students know how to upload their required
work before the session ends. A proper workflow minimizes ambiguity and helps prevent
lost or misfiled submissions.
Evidence handling is equally important. The system should maintain structured link-
age between the submission, the student identity, the assigned exam, the session timing,
and the relevant event history. This evidence-oriented requirement supports academic
fairness because it enables instructors or reviewers to interpret a submission within its
full examination context. For example, if a technical interruption or suspicious event
occurred, the evidence record should help explain that context during review.
2.2.5 Reporting and Post-Exam Review Requirements
The project must also support the post-exam phase, which is frequently neglected
in simpler exam systems. After an examination has concluded, academic staff often
CHAPTER 2. SOFTWARE REQUIREMENTS SPECIFICATION18
need more than raw student files. They need structured summaries showing attendance,
submission completion, irregularity history, and session-level observations. Therefore, the
system shall generate reports that help instructors and administrators review the exam
in an organized manner.
Such reports are valuable for several reasons. They support internal quality review,
help resolve student concerns, and improve future exam planning. They also elevate the
system from being a real-time monitoring tool to being a broader management platform
for practical examination governance.
2.3 Non-Functional Requirements
Non-functional requirements define the quality conditions under which the functional
services must operate. In a project concerned with secure and fair academic assessment,
non-functional properties are not secondary. If the system is difficult to use, invigilators
may ignore it during the exam. If it performs poorly, the dashboard may become op-
erationally irrelevant. If it is insecure, confidence in the entire assessment process may
decline. For these reasons, the proposed system must satisfy multiple quality dimensions
simultaneously.
2.3.1 Software Quality Attributes
The main software quality attributes for the proposed system are security, reliability,
usability, performance, maintainability, and data integrity. Security is essential because
the platform manages identities, exam schedules, student records, and potentially sen-
sitive monitoring evidence. Reliability is equally important because a lab exam cannot
pause simply because the system behaves inconsistently. Usability matters because ad-
ministrators and teachers must interact with the platform under time pressure, while
students need a clear and low-confusion exam interface.
Performance is also important because the system must respond quickly during lo-
gin, attendance verification, exam launch, live monitoring, and file submission. Main-
tainability is relevant because the project may later be refined, extended, or integrated
with broader institutional workflows. Data integrity remains critical throughout because
schedules, attendance status, incident logs, results, and reports must remain consistent
and auditable.
2.3.2 Other Non-Functional Requirements
Other relevant non-functional requirements include portability, privacy sensitivity,
compatibility with university lab settings, and support for structured backup practices.
The proposed system should operate within the technological realities of academic com-
puter labs, where hardware and operating environments may vary. It should also preserve
logs and exam records in a consistent manner so that important information is not lost
due to technical disruption or later review needs.
Privacy considerations are particularly important when user activity, event logs, or
submission-related evidence are retained. The system should therefore support responsi-
ble access control and discourage unnecessary exposure of sensitive student-related infor-
mation. In addition, the platform should remain easy to document and explain within
an academic environment, because FYP success depends not only on implementation but
also on clarity of presentation and justification.

2.4 Requirement Gathering Techniques Used
The requirements for the proposed system were gathered through a multi-perspective
process because lab examination problems are operational, academic, and technical at
the same time. No single elicitation technique could capture the full picture. The project
therefore used interviews, prototyping, and brainstorming sessions to define realistic re-
quirements.
2.4.0.1 InterviewsInterviews are a direct requirement elicitation technique in which
stakeholders are asked structured questions to explain their current workflow, difficulties,
and expectations. In this project, interviews were used to understand how teachers and
academic staff currently schedule practical exams, supervise students, collect files, and
review incidents.
Two key questions guided this technique. The first question asked what difficulties
arise during practical lab exams when many students must be supervised at the same
time. The second question asked what information teachers and administrators need
after the exam for attendance verification, result handling, and academic review.
The outcomes of the interviews were clear and actionable. First, they highlighted
CHAPTER 2. SOFTWARE REQUIREMENTS SPECIFICATION20
the need for role-based access and controlled scheduling. Second, they showed that live
monitoring and incident logging are essential for exam oversight. Third, they confirmed
that teachers need structured reporting after the session rather than scattered manual
records.
2.4.0.2 PrototypingPrototyping is a requirement elicitation technique in which early
interface sketches or mock workflows are shown to stakeholders so that hidden usability
issues can be discovered before full implementation. In this project, simple interface ideas
for login, scheduling, monitoring, and submission were considered to refine the system
flow.
Two key questions guided this technique. The first question asked whether the pro-
posed dashboards and screens were understandable enough for use during time-sensitive
exam conditions. The second question asked whether the sequence from login to submis-
sion matched the actual working pattern of a lab exam.
The outcomes of prototyping improved the requirement quality in three ways. First,
it clarified the need for a simpler teacher dashboard with visible student status. Second,
it confirmed that students need a guided exam flow with clear timing and submission
prompts. Third, it exposed the importance of integrating attendance, monitoring, and
reporting instead of treating them as unrelated screens.
2.4.0.3 Brainstorming Session 1: Academic Workflow DiscussionBrainstorm-
ing is a collaborative elicitation technique in which team members discuss ideas, prob-
lems, and possible solutions in multiple sessions to identify realistic and prioritized system
requirements. In the first session, the discussion focused on the complete academic work-
flow, starting from exam creation and ending with final result review.
Two key questions guided this session. The first question asked which steps of the
current practical exam workflow consume the most manual effort. The second question
asked which modules are essential if the system is to provide value from the very first
prototype.
The outcomes of the first brainstorming session established three priorities. First,
exam scheduling and role management were identified as foundation modules. Second,
lab allocation and attendance were recognized as necessary controls for a real computer-
lab environment. Third, the team agreed that result management must remain connected
to the earlier monitoring and submission steps.
2.4.0.4 Brainstorming Session 2: Monitoring and Reporting DiscussionThe
second brainstorming session focused specifically on monitoring, identity verification, inci-
dent handling, and reporting. This session explored how AI-based alerts, face recognition,
and evidence logging could be made useful within the practical limitations of an FYP
project.
Two key questions guided this session. The first question asked which monitoring
events should be recorded as meaningful evidence during a live exam. The second question
asked what type of final reports would help teachers and administrators review the session
efficiently.
The outcomes of the second brainstorming session refined the advanced modules of
the project. First, the team prioritized face recognition before exam access rather than
after the session. Second, it defined incident logs and alerts as central monitoring outputs.
Third, it confirmed that attendance, incidents, and results should appear in one reporting
layer so that post-exam review remains coherent.

The Software Project Plan explains how the AI-Driven Lab Exam Monitoring and Man-

year project lifecycle. In software projects that involve multiple user roles, security-
sensitive assessment workflows, real-time monitoring, evidence capture, and reporting
obligations, planning is not a formality. It is the mechanism that holds the project to-
gether. Without a clear project plan, separate modules may be built in isolation, key
deadlines may be missed, quality checks may become inconsistent, and documentation
may fail to reflect the actual development journey.
For the proposed project, the plan is particularly important because the system inte-
grates administrative control, invigilation support, and student-facing exam interaction
within a single platform. Such integration creates dependency chains across modules.
Scheduling logic affects monitoring. Monitoring affects evidence handling. Evidence han-
dling affects reports. Reports affect post-exam academic decisions. Therefore, the project
must be guided through a structured plan that balances ambition, feasibility, academic
deadlines, and iterative improvement.
This chapter describes the deliverables, management approach, scheduling logic, man-
agerial process assumptions, and risk management strategy that will guide the project.
The overall aim is to ensure that development remains practical, measurable, and aca-
demically defensible from the first sprint to the final presentation.
3.1 Deliverables of the Project
Project deliverables are the concrete outputs that demonstrate whether the project is
progressing in a meaningful and measurable way. In the context of a final year project,
deliverables are not limited to the final software prototype. They also include planning
artifacts, design descriptions, chapter documentation, diagrams, testing evidence, and
presentation material. These deliverables help the team measure progress, align expecta-
tions with the supervisor, and ensure that the project remains grounded in documented
academic work rather than ad hoc development.
For the proposed AI-Driven Lab Exam Monitoring and Management System, deliver-
ables are especially important because the project combines practical software construc-
tion with report-based academic justification. A deliverable must therefore contribute
either to system realization, project control, or academic evaluation. The following sub-
sections explain the major deliverables in a more detailed manner.

requirement specification, workflow interpretation, and chapter documentation produced
during the early stage of the project. These deliverables are important because they
establish why the project is needed and what the system is expected to accomplish.
They also act as a baseline against which later design and implementation decisions
can be judged. If the analysis deliverables are weak, later development may become
technically active but academically unfocused.

3.2 Project Risk Management
Risk management is the process through which potential problems are identified, as-
sessed, and prepared for before they seriously affect project success. In the proposed
AI-Driven Lab Exam Monitoring and Management System, risk management is espe-
cially important because the project involves technical integration, real-time workflow
assumptions, multiple stakeholder expectations, and strict academic deadlines. Risks
do not need to become actual failures if they are recognized early and managed in a
disciplined way.
3.2.1 Risk Identification
Several categories of risk may affect the project. Technical risks include integration
difficulties between modules, instability in monitoring features, or data-handling com-
plexity. Schedule risks include delays in design completion, incomplete sprint outcomes,
or documentation falling behind implementation. Academic risks include misunderstand-
ing stakeholder needs, insufficient justification in the report, or weak alignment between
written chapters and the evolving prototype. Resource risks may include limited access
to realistic testing conditions, hardware constraints, or tool-related limitations.
3.2.2 Risk Analysis
Risk analysis helps the team determine which risks deserve the most attention. A risk
is not important only because it is possible; it becomes important when its likelihood
combines with meaningful impact. For example, a small wording correction in a chapter

may be likely but low impact, whereas instability in a monitoring feature during late-stage
integration may be both moderately likely and highly disruptive. The team therefore
evaluates risks in terms of both probability and seriousness.

3.2.3 Risk Mitigation Plan
Risk mitigation involves planning responses before problems grow. Requirement in-
stability can be reduced through early scope clarification and regular supervisor review.
Integration risk can be reduced through modular development and early testing of inter-

incrementally throughout the project rather than postponing them. Testing constraints
can be reduced through scenario-based validation even when full real-world deployment
is not possible. Time pressure can be reduced through milestone tracking and careful
attention to critical-path activities.

Risk management is not a one-time exercise performed only at the proposal stage.

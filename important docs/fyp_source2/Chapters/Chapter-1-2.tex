\section{Product Scope}

The scope of the proposed AI-Driven Lab Exam Monitoring and Management System is limited to the planning, controlled execution, monitoring, submission handling, and reporting of practical laboratory examinations in an academic environment \cite{scope1}. The proposed system covers twelve project modules: user authentication, role management, exam scheduling, question and instruction management, laboratory allocation, workstation control, student attendance, face recognition, AI-based monitoring, incident logging, result management, and reporting. Within this scope, the product delivers five core feature groups: secure role-based access for administrators, teachers, and students; scheduled exam creation with lab and machine mapping; monitored exam execution with face verification and AI-supported alerts; organized submission, result, and evidence handling; and administrative reporting for review, audit, and decision support.

\subsection{Existing System Description}

The current practical laboratory examination environment in many academic institutions is still managed through a mix of manual supervision, isolated software tools, verbal coordination, and post-exam documentation. Instructors often prepare the exam separately, communicate schedules through informal channels, verify attendance manually, collect files from multiple sources, and later reconstruct incidents from memory or incomplete notes. This fragmented setup becomes difficult to manage when the number of students, workstations, and simultaneous lab sessions increases.

The following application review highlights ten widely used systems related to online assessment, secure testing, plagiarism analysis, and learning management. Each review shows that existing tools solve selected parts of the examination problem, but none of them fully combines lab scheduling, identity verification, machine control, AI-assisted monitoring, result management, and institutional reporting in one workflow tailored for supervised lab exams.

\subsubsection{Respondus LockDown Browser}
Respondus LockDown Browser is a dedicated assessment browser that helps institutions protect online tests by restricting access to other applications, browser functions, printing options, and copy-paste behavior during an exam session \cite{respondus1}. It is widely used in universities because it integrates with popular learning platforms and focuses on maintaining a controlled test-taking interface.

\paragraph{Features}
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
\item Integrates with major learning management systems.
\item Restricts opening of new tabs and applications.
\item Disables printing and copy-paste actions.
\item Supports controlled quiz launch processes.
\item Offers add-on monitoring support through Respondus Monitor.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Limitations}
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
\item Does not provide complete lab scheduling workflows.
\item Offers limited institution-specific workstation mapping.
\item Does not focus on result and report management for lab exams.
\item Incident handling is narrower than a dedicated invigilator dashboard.
\item Practical file-based lab workflows require additional systems.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Safe Exam Browser}

\subsection{Future System Usage Analysis}
The future system is expected to be used as an operational platform across the complete life cycle of practical laboratory examinations. Before the exam, administrators and teachers will use it to configure user roles, define examination sessions, assign laboratories, map students to approved machines, publish instructions, and confirm readiness. During the exam, students will use it to access the scheduled assessment under controlled conditions, while teachers and invigilators will rely on the monitoring dashboard to observe status changes, review alerts, and intervene when necessary. After the exam, teachers and administrators will use it to review submissions, examine incidents, verify attendance, manage results, and generate structured reports.

In a practical institutional deployment, the system will be especially valuable in courses involving programming, software construction, data structures, operating systems, database laboratories, networking exercises, and related disciplines where each student works at a computer and produces digital outputs. It can help standardize operational conduct across sections and semesters and reduce dependence on improvised processes.

Another important future-use dimension concerns accountability and evidence. The proposed system will support a disciplined evidence trail through structured logs and linked session records, strengthening institutional memory and supporting academic disputes, result review, and quality evaluation.
